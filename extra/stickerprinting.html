I did start out with ordering from a couple of professional places in 2017, wrote up notes on it here:

I didn't like the result, most professional places (except for giclee specialists) use toners to shorten the cost and of course are limited to the typical four inks, CMYK.

I really wanted to get the colours right, so I got a Canon PIXMA Pro 1 which houses twelve different coloured inks, and they're archival pigments rather than the typical dye inks, which is why the colours come out so vibrant.

The downside is the inks are $420 a set.

Only Canon and Epson hit that quality in their prosumer lines of printers, both with different issues. At the time Epson's inks were a nice bit cheaper for the same volume of ink at almost the same quality but famously lots of their high end printers die a little after a year right after their warrantee expires (and I decided I wasn't going to gamble on that).





And the Canon has a problem of flushing the ink if you stop printing for 2 days, in order to keep the heads clean, so you really need to keep on top of continuously having stuff ready to print, hence why I ended up just fucking around with stuff like the tiny pokemon stickers for shits and giggles.

So it costs me about $40 of flushed ink to do a "run" of printing, where I'll print stuff every day for as many consecutive days as I can, then I'll let the printer sit however many months without use, so it's currently out of commission until I get another large job to do. If I can maximise the amount of sheets completed per flush, those tiny pokemon sticker sheets cost about an estimated $3.50 to $4 each in materials for instance.

The vinyl sheets I used were from PCX, an industrial print supply company who tragically folded a couple of years ago. I tried out a bunch of cotton papers and vinyl sheets and sticker papers from a number of suppliers, and they were by far the best. The brightening chemicals (called "optical brighteners" in giclee circles) that they use in the sheets contributes to the vibrance, but does mean they'll fade faster than archival papers, so if you want stickers for outdoor purposes you'd cover them in a UV protective adhesive over the top of the printed vinyl, like you'll see in stickers from the high-end print houses.

It initially cost about $300 for various samples of different sticker papers, vinyls and even plastic holographic film to work out what worked best, so I'm not in any hurry to do that again. ðŸ˜¬

I should run a workshop on how to do it one of these days and get through the rest of the materials.
